# Fig — Brand narrative

---

## The story

The apple gets all the credit, but it was the fig.

In Genesis, after the first two people became aware of each other — truly aware, not just coexisting — the first thing they did was reach for fig leaves. Not to hide. To share. The fig leaf was the first gesture of mutual care between two people who had just realized they were in this together.

That's what Fig is. Not a tool for managing your relationship. A way of seeing each other's day — when it's heavy, when it's light, when your rhythms overlap and a window opens for the two of you.

Cut a fig in half and you'll see two symmetric chambers, each with its own pattern of seeds, sharing a common center. That's the app. Your day on one side, theirs on the other, and the moments you share running down the middle.

---

## Positioning

**One-line:** A beautiful, purpose-built calendar for two.

**Elevator (30 seconds):** Most shared calendars pull in your Google and Outlook and stack everything on top of each other. Fig doesn't. It gives you and your partner a split-screen timeline — your day on one side, theirs on the other, shared moments in the center. You can see the shape of each other's day at a glance: when they're packed, when they're free, when you're both free at the same time. It's not a relationship management app. It's a calendar that was designed, from the ground up, for exactly two people.

**What Fig is not:**

- Not a relationship hub (no chat, no shared grocery lists, no date night AI)
- Not a calendar aggregator (does not overlay Google/Apple/Outlook)
- Not a family organizer (not built for 5 people and a color for the dog)

**What Fig is:**

- A calendar experience you want to open
- A way to feel the rhythm of someone else's day alongside yours
- The only calendar with a purpose-built two-person timeline

### Description

You already have a calendar. It's fine. It tells you where you need to be and when.

Fig is different. Fig shows you what your day looks like next to theirs.

Open the app and you'll see a split-screen timeline — your schedule on one side, your partner's on the other, and shared events running down the center. Not stacked on top of each other. Not color-coded into a single list. Side by side, the way you actually live.

That changes how you think about your time together. Instead of asking "are you free Tuesday?", you can see that their afternoon is packed but the evening opens up at six. Instead of guessing when to suggest dinner, you can spot the gap where both your days exhale at the same time.

**Your day, their day, your day together.**

Fig gives every event one of three levels: just yours, visible to them, or shared. Private events stay in your column. Shared events span the center. You decide what they see — and they decide what you see.

**Find time together.**

One tap highlights the windows where you're both free. No back-and-forth. No "let me check my calendar." The answer is right there on the screen.

**Built to feel good.**

Fig is not a spreadsheet with a skin. The timeline, the interactions, the way events snap into place — every detail is designed to make scheduling feel less like work and more like something you want to do. Drag to create an event. Drag to move it. Pull an edge to make it longer. Fifteen-minute precision, zero friction.

**What Fig doesn't do.**

Fig is not trying to be your relationship's operating system. No chat. No shared grocery lists. No AI suggesting date ideas. Just a calendar — the best calendar two people have ever shared.

### Section 1: The problem

Every shared calendar app does the same thing: it pulls in your Google Calendar and your partner's and stacks them on top of each other. You get a pile of color-coded blocks and the vague hope that you won't double-book date night.

Fig starts from a different premise. What if a calendar for two people actually looked like two people?

### Section 2: The split screen

Open Fig and you'll see your day running down the left side and theirs running down the right. Shared events — dinners, trips, the things you're doing together — span the center.

It's not a feature. It's the entire product. Every interaction, every visual decision, every line of code was built around this one idea: your day, side by side with theirs.

### Section 3: Three levels of sharing

Not everything needs to be shared. Fig gives every event one of three settings:

**Only Mine** — Only you see it. It lives in your column and nowhere else.

**Visible** — They can see it in your column, but it's yours. "I have a dentist appointment at 3" without making it a group event.

**Ours** — A shared event that spans the center lane. Both of you can edit it, move it, or delete it.

You control the boundary. They control theirs.

### Section 4: Find time together

Tap once and Fig highlights the windows where both of your schedules are open. No more "when works for you?" texts. The answer is already on the screen.

### Section 5: Why it's called Fig

Cut a fig in half and you'll see two symmetric chambers sharing a common center — each with its own structure, its own seeds, but part of the same fruit. That's what this calendar looks like. That's what it's for.

The fig was also the first thing two people ever shared. Not the apple. The fig.

## Voice guidelines

**Warm, not sweet.** Fig's voice sounds like a thoughtful person talking to another adult, not a brand talking to a "user." No exclamation marks. No "you've got this!" energy. No emoji in product copy.

**Confident, not loud.** The product makes a strong claim — "the best calendar two people have ever shared" — and then lets the experience back it up. No superlatives beyond that one line. No "revolutionary" or "game-changing."

**Short.** If a sentence can lose a word, lose it. If a paragraph can lose a sentence, lose it. The product is minimal; the voice matches.

**Direct.** Say what the thing does. "See each other's day" is better than "Stay connected with your partner's schedule through seamless calendar integration."

**The line between confidence and arrogance:** Fig can say "most shared calendars just overlay Google Calendar — Fig doesn't." Fig should not say "other apps are doing it wrong." Describe what you are, not what they aren't.
