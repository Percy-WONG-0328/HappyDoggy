# Fig / HappyDoggy Strategy Log

Date: 2026-06-30

This document records the product strategy discussion around renaming HappyDoggy, repositioning the product, assessing Cupla as a competitor, and reducing legal and market risk. It is intentionally redacted for public GitHub use: personal addresses, order numbers, emails, and other private data are not included.

## 1. Starting Point

The original product name, HappyDoggy, does not fit the product. It feels too playful and unrelated to a serious, intimate shared calendar. The product itself is not a generic calendar. It is a two-person shared time interface.

Two candidate names were discussed:

- Fig
- Guava

Fig was preferred because it is short, mature, memorable, and symbolically aligned with the product's structure: two separate sides sharing a common center. The biblical fig reference can be used lightly, but should not become the main selling point.

Recommended naming:

- Product name: Fig
- App Store style name: Fig: Calendar for Two
- Primary line: See each other's day.

## 2. Core Product Positioning

The product should not compete as a general calendar. Google Calendar, Apple Calendar, Outlook, and other calendar tools already own that space.

The sharper positioning:

Fig is a quiet calendar for two people.

It helps two people see each other's day, find shared time, and keep personal boundaries.

The product is not:

- A relationship assistant
- A family organizer
- A shared chores app
- A grocery list app
- A date planner
- A general calendar aggregator

The product is:

- A split-screen timeline for two
- A way to see the rhythm of each other's day
- A lightweight shared time space
- A tool for finding time together without asking repeatedly
- A privacy-aware calendar with clear boundaries

## 3. Target Users

The product should start narrow, not broad.

Recommended first target segments:

1. Long-distance or semi-long-distance couples
   - They need to know when the other person is free.
   - Time zones, sleep windows, call windows, and visit planning matter.

2. University and graduate-student couples
   - They have classes, exams, clubs, internships, and flexible but fragmented schedules.
   - They may have lower paid conversion, but stronger sharing and word-of-mouth potential.

3. Young working couples in major cities
   - They have higher willingness to pay.
   - Their pain is unstable work schedules, missed windows, and frequent "when are you free?" friction.

Avoid starting with:

- Families with children
- Household chore management
- Team scheduling
- General friend groups

Those directions pull the product toward heavier competitors and weaken differentiation.

## 4. Cupla Competitive Assessment

Cupla is a direct and serious competitor. Its positioning overlaps with the original idea, including a two-person calendar/timeline experience. However, Cupla has expanded beyond calendar into a broader relationship assistant.

Cupla's current direction:

- Shared couples calendar
- To-dos
- Groceries
- Wishlists
- Date planner
- AI assistant
- Relationship mental-load reduction

Cupla's apparent growth channels:

- App Store and Google Play search optimization
- SEO content targeting couple calendar queries
- Competitor-comparison content such as Cupla vs TimeTree
- Couple invite loop
- Founder story: built by a real couple for couples
- Social proof through reviews, download claims, and impact reports

Cupla's likely customer base:

- Busy couples
- Married or cohabiting partners
- Parents
- Long-distance couples
- iOS / Android mixed-device couples

Public pricing signals:

- Freemium model
- Monthly subscription around USD 5-8 in some listings
- Annual subscription around USD 35-60 in some listings
- Pricing appears to have varied across time, region, or experiments

Public revenue data was not found. Any revenue estimate remains speculative.

## 5. Differentiation Strategy

Do not build a lightweight Cupla clone.

Cupla's territory:

Manage your relationship.

Fig's territory:

See each other's rhythm.

Cupla is heavier and broader. Fig should be lighter, quieter, and more focused.

Differentiation pillars:

1. Quietness
   - No relationship dashboard.
   - No emotional scorekeeping.
   - No excessive modules.

2. Boundaries
   - Mine / Visible / Ours should be central to the product.
   - Personal time should remain personal.

3. Time perception
   - Show the shape of each other's day.
   - Make shared windows visible.

4. Low-friction input
   - Manual event creation alone is not enough long term.
   - Calendar import, screenshot parsing, class schedule parsing, and work-shift parsing are important.

5. Long-distance use cases
   - Time zones
   - Sleep windows
   - Call windows
   - Visit countdowns

## 6. China Market Monetization

The product should not copy the US-style subscription-first strategy. Chinese users often resist paying recurring fees for small utility software. They may still pay for strong outcomes, emotional value, identity, content, service, convenience, or relationship-related value.

Recommended China monetization approach:

- Free core product
- Low-price annual couple plan
- Early lifetime pass
- Festival pricing around 520, Qixi, Valentine's Day, back-to-school season, and long-distance reunion moments

Possible pricing:

- Free: core two-person day view, basic events, basic sharing
- Plus: RMB 52/year per couple
- Early lifetime: RMB 88 or RMB 128 per couple
- Festival lifetime: RMB 131.4 per couple
- Small emotional purchases: RMB 5.20 for special themes or reports

Do not lead with "productivity software."

Better China-facing framing:

Fig is a two-person time space.

It helps you see when the other person is busy, when you are both free, and how to stay close without constantly asking.

Potential China acquisition channels:

- WeChat Mini Program for low-friction entry
- Xiaohongshu content
- Couple invite loop
- Student communities
- Long-distance relationship content
- Festival campaigns

## 7. Legal / IP Initial Screening

This was a non-lawyer preliminary public search, not formal legal advice.

### USPTO Trademark

A relevant US trademark record was found:

- Mark: CUPLA
- Owner: CUPLA LIMITED
- Serial number: 79374730
- Status: Live / Registered
- Filing date: 2023-04-12
- US registration date: 2025-01-07
- Classes: 009, 042, 045
- Filing basis: Madrid Protocol / Section 66(a)
- International registration number: 1741372

The mark covers software and services related to shared calendars, SaaS, relationship/time management, and social/dating-related services.

Practical conclusion:

Do not use Cupla, Coupla, Couple Calendar, or any confusingly similar name.

Fig is meaningfully different from Cupla as a mark.

### WIPO Madrid

WIPO Madrid record 1741372 was identified for CUPLA.

Observed designated territories include:

- AU
- CA
- EM
- GB
- JP
- KR
- SG
- US

CN was not observed in the Madrid designation list during the preliminary check.

Practical conclusion:

The WIPO Madrid route does not appear to directly designate China, but this does not fully rule out a separate China filing.

### China CNIPA

The CNIPA / China trademark web search page was not successfully queried during this session because the dynamic page timed out. Public search-index checks for CUPLA LIMITED, CUPLA, and related Chinese trademark terms did not show a clear China record.

Practical conclusion:

Before a China launch, run a dedicated CNIPA trademark search for:

- CUPLA
- CUPLA LIMITED
- Fig
- The chosen Chinese name
- Related Chinese translations

### Patents / GUI / Design Rights

Searches for Cupla Limited patents, Cupla design patents, Cupla GUI design rights, WIPO Hague design records, and obvious patent-index results did not reveal a clear Cupla GUI design or patent covering the two-person timeline view.

Practical conclusion:

No obvious public record was found showing that Cupla has locked down the two-person timeline GUI via design patent or international design registration.

However, this is only a preliminary search. The safest path is still to make Fig visually and verbally distinct.

## 8. Risk Assessment

Trademark risk:

- Low if using Fig
- High if using Cupla-like naming

GUI/design risk:

- Low to medium-low for the abstract two-person timeline concept
- Higher if the visual implementation looks too close to Cupla

Market perception risk:

- Significant if Fig looks like a "Chinese Cupla clone"
- Lower if Fig owns a distinct quiet, minimal, boundary-first identity

Platform complaint risk:

- More realistic than a full lawsuit
- App Store, Google Play, or social platforms may respond to complaints faster than courts

## 9. Design Safety Checklist

To reduce legal and market risk:

- Keep all original design files and git history.
- Save the early PRD and product discussion records.
- Avoid Cupla-like names, subtitles, and feature wording.
- Avoid "relationship assistant" language.
- Avoid copying Cupla's screenshots, onboarding, pricing page, app icon, color palette, or information architecture.
- Redesign the main view so it is not merely a recolored Cupla-like timeline.
- Make Mine / Visible / Ours a distinctive interaction model.
- Build a unique visual system around quietness, rhythm, boundaries, and shared windows.
- Create a side-by-side similarity audit before launch.

## 10. Immediate Product Direction

The next product strategy should be:

1. Rename HappyDoggy to Fig.
2. Reposition from "couples calendar" to "quiet calendar for two."
3. Focus the first market on long-distance, student, and young working couples.
4. Build the timeline experience deeply rather than adding many relationship modules.
5. Add import and parsing features to reduce manual input.
6. Prepare China-first monetization with low-price annual/lifetime plans.
7. Keep legal risk low by making the product look, sound, and feel distinct from Cupla.
